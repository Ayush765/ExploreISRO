Mars Orbiter Mission





India's Mars Orbiter Mission (Mangalyaan), launched on November 5, 2013, aboard the PSLV-C25 rocket, represents a groundbreaking milestone in India's space exploration journey. The mission was not only a technological triumph but also a testament to India's cost-effective approach to interplanetary exploration. The primary objectives of Mangalyaan were threefold: to showcase India's technological capabilities, conduct scientific investigations of the Martian atmosphere, surface, and mineralogy, and explore Martian morphology.

What sets Mangalyaan apart is its remarkable cost-effectiveness, making India the first Asian nation and the first globally to successfully enter Martian orbit on its maiden attempt. The mission's total cost was approximately $74 million, a fraction of the expenses associated with similar missions conducted by other space agencies. This cost-effectiveness garnered international attention and solidified India's reputation as a pragmatic and resource-efficient player in space exploration.

The orbiter is equipped with five scientific instruments, each contributing to the mission's success. The Mars Color Camera (MCC) captured high-resolution images of the Martian surface, while the Methane Sensor for Mars (MSM) played a crucial role in detecting methane levels in the Martian atmosphere, providing valuable insights into the planet's geology and potential signs of life.

Mangalyaan's success brought India global acclaim for its technological achievements. As of my last update in January 2022, the orbiter remains operational, continuing to send data back to Earth. The Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) manages this data, contributing to ongoing research on Mars.

Key Specifications and Mission Details

Mission Launch Date Landing Date Mission Objectives
Mars Science Laboratory (Curiosity Rover) Nov 26, 2011 Aug 6, 2012 Assess whether Mars had an environment capable of supporting microbial life; analyze the Martian surface.
Mars Atmosphere and Volatile Evolution (MAVEN) Nov 18, 2013 Sep 21, 2014 Study the Martian atmosphere, its evolution, and the role of solar wind in the loss of Martian atmosphere.
InSight May 5, 2018 Nov 26, 2018 Investigate the interior of Mars, study seismic activity (Marsquakes), and analyze heat flow from the planet.
Perseverance Rover (Mars 2020) Jul 30, 2020 Feb 18, 2021 Search for signs of past microbial life, collect and cache Martian soil and rock samples, test new technologies.
